Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock (1903):

The Disasters of Plenary Power

Overview

At the turn of the 20th century, as the USA sought to expand its lands, Congress began using its power to further encroach on Native lands. One such case, Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock (1903), sought to fight against these unjust seizures. Ultimately, the case gave Congress complete power to manage Native lands however they pleased without judicial oversight. This empowered Congress to be more aggressive in its land seizures and completely nullified the treaties whose power was already beginning to wane. Built upon the Marshall Trilogy, United States v. Kagama (1886), and the colonialist attitude enforced by the Dawes Act, the plenary power endowed by the Court stood as a threat to Native sovereignty, which remains in the law.

Plenary Power

The doctrine of plenary power is antithetical to the design of checks and balances proposed within the Constitution, yet it persists in law to this day. While it may not be used to remove Natives from their lands, it serves as a justification for much of federal Indian policy, including the Indian Child Welfare Act and around 111 cases (Ishtani and Fay). While Congress has additionally pulled its power from other parts of the Constitution, its unchecked plenary powers over Native affairs remain an underlying danger for Native sovereignty. Fortunately, all is not so downhearted as continued activism and decisions by the Court have questioned plenary power. Examining the plenary power doctrine has resulted in the paradoxical conclusion that Congress' power is plenary but not absolute, as it is constrained by the Constitution (Steele). However, these limits in actuality do little to prevent Congress' authority. Therefore, as long as the doctrine of plenary power exists within the law, permanent vigilance must be retained to prevent the violation of the rights and well-being of Native Americans.
